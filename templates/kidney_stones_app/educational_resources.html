Overview of Kidney Stone Pathogenesis

Kidney stone disease, also known as nephrolithiasis or urolithiasis, is a disorder in which urinary solutes precipitate to form aggregates of crystalline material in the urinary space. The formation is a complex biologic process involving a balance of crystallization inhibitors and promoters, urine volume, and urine pH.

Common Stone Types

  • Most common type. Predisposing factors: low urine volume, hypercalciuria, hypocitraturia, hyperoxaluria.
  • Chronic Management: Increase urine volume (~2.5 L/d), restrict sodium, optimize calcium intake, thiazide if hypercalciuric, potassium citrate if hypocitraturic, oxalate restriction for significant hyperoxaluria, restrict animal protein and sucrose/fructose.

  • Less soluble at higher pH. Often with hypocitraturia.
  • Chronic Management: Address reversible factors (e.g., discontinue topiramate/acetazolamide), increase urine volume, restrict sodium, thiazide if hypercalciuric, treat acidosis with potassium citrate, consider potassium chloride if potassium deficiency.

  • Radiolucent. Low urine pH, low volume, hyperuricosuria are key factors.
  • Chronic Management: Raise urine pH to 6.5-7.0 with alkali therapy, increase urine volume, lower animal protein intake, consider allopurinol if hyperuricosuric and stones persist.

  • Result from chronic UTI by urease-producing organisms. Can form staghorn calculi.
  • Chronic Management: Eradicate infection, early surgical removal, increase urine volume, consider urease inhibitors.

  • Rare genetic disorder. Cystine is poorly soluble at typical urine pH.
  • Chronic Management: Increase urine volume, restrict sodium, reduce animal protein, alkali therapy to maintain pH 7.0-7.5, consider thiol drugs if stones persist.

Associated Systemic Conditions

  • Metabolic Syndrome/Type 2 Diabetes: Linked to uric acid and calcium oxalate stones due to acidic urine and other metabolic derangements.
  • Osteoporosis/Bone Health: Skeleton can be a source of excessive urinary calcium.
  • Malabsorption Syndromes: (e.g., Crohn's, Gastric Bypass, Celiac, Cystic Fibrosis) predispose to calcium oxalate stones (enteric hyperoxaluria).

  • Gout: Doubles risk of nephrolithiasis, often linked to metabolic syndrome.
  • Primary Hyperparathyroidism: Causes hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria, leading to calcium stones.
